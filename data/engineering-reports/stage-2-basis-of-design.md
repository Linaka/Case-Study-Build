# Example Stage 2 Basis of Design Engineering Report Structure

This structure assumes a UK/RIBA-style project where **Stage 2 = Concept Design**. The RIBA Plan of Work describes Stage 2 as the point where the design concept is established and tested against the client’s brief, vision and budget; it is not yet a fully coordinated technical design stage.

## 1. Document Control

1.1 Report title
1.2 Project name and number
1.3 Client / end user
1.4 Design team members
1.5 Revision history
1.6 Status of issue, for example: draft, client review, planning support, Stage 2 close-out
1.7 Author, checker and approver
1.8 Distribution list

---

## 2. Executive Summary

2.1 Purpose of the report
2.2 Summary of the Stage 2 engineering concept
2.3 Key design drivers
2.4 Principal engineering decisions made during Stage 2
2.5 Key risks, assumptions and dependencies
2.6 Recommended next steps for Stage 3

This section should give a non-technical reader a clear view of what the engineering strategy is, why it has been selected, and what needs to be resolved next.

---

## 3. Project Overview

3.1 Project description
3.2 Site location and context
3.3 Project objectives
3.4 Client requirements
3.5 Stakeholder requirements
3.6 Stage 1 information carried forward
3.7 Scope of this Stage 2 report
3.8 Items outside the current scope

Example wording:

> This report records the Stage 2 engineering basis of design for the proposed development. It identifies the design criteria, assumptions, engineering strategies, constraints and risks that will inform the coordinated Stage 3 design.

---

## 4. Basis of Design

4.1 Design brief and performance objectives
4.2 Applicable design standards, codes and guidance
4.3 Planning, statutory and authority requirements
4.4 Environmental and sustainability objectives
4.5 Resilience, adaptability and future flexibility requirements
4.6 Operational and maintenance assumptions
4.7 Design life assumptions
4.8 Occupancy, use and loading assumptions
4.9 Interfaces with architecture, landscape, interiors and specialist consultants

This section is useful for capturing the cross-disciplinary basis before the design moves into greater spatial coordination.

---

## 5. Existing Information Reviewed

5.1 Client brief
5.2 Site surveys
5.3 Topographical information
5.4 Utilities records
5.5 Ground investigation information
5.6 Flood risk / drainage information
5.7 Existing building information, where applicable
5.8 Planning history
5.9 Environmental data
5.10 Previous feasibility or options studies
5.11 Assumptions made due to missing information

A simple table works well here:

| Information reviewed | Source | Date | Status | Key relevance |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Topographical survey | Surveyor | DD/MM/YYYY | Received | Levels, boundaries, existing features |
| Utility records | Statutory providers | DD/MM/YYYY | Partial | Existing service constraints |
| Stage 1 brief | Client team | DD/MM/YYYY | Approved | Project requirements |

---

## 6. Site Constraints and Opportunities

6.1 Site access and logistics
6.2 Existing utilities and diversions
6.3 Ground conditions
6.4 Flooding and drainage constraints
6.5 Adjacent buildings and infrastructure
6.6 Rights of way, easements or third-party constraints
6.7 Environmental constraints
6.8 Planning constraints
6.9 Opportunities for efficiency, reuse or low-carbon design

This section should connect site realities to design decisions. For example, if a basement is avoided due to groundwater risk, or if plant is located at roof level due to ground-floor constraints, record that logic here.

---

## 7. Engineering Design Principles

7.1 Safety by design
7.2 Simplicity and robustness
7.3 Whole-life carbon and energy performance
7.4 Maintainability and access
7.5 Flexibility for future change
7.6 Buildability and phasing
7.7 Passive design principles
7.8 Integration with architectural intent

This section can be short, but it is valuable because it explains the engineering philosophy behind the concept.

# Discipline Sections

The following sections can be used for a multidisciplinary engineering report. Remove or expand disciplines depending on the project scope.

## 8. Civil Engineering Strategy

8.3 Earthworks assumptions
8.4 Access roads and external hardstanding
8.5 External works design principles
8.6 Below-ground constraints
8.7 Interface with landscape and public realm
8.8 Civil engineering risks and next-stage actions

Possible Stage 2 outputs:

- Preliminary levels strategy
- Cut-and-fill assumptions

- Key constraints plan
- Civil engineering design risk register

---

## 9. Drainage and Water Management Strategy

9.1 Existing drainage context

9.5 Attenuation assumptions
9.6 Discharge rate assumptions
9.7 Flood risk considerations
9.8 Blue-green infrastructure opportunities
9.9 Drainage authority engagement
9.10 Risks and next-stage actions

Example content to include:

| Design item | Stage 2 assumption | To be confirmed at Stage 3 |
|---|---|---|
| Surface water discharge | Restricted discharge likely required | Authority agreement |
| Attenuation | Below-ground tank or landscape-based storage | Spatial coordination |
| Foul drainage | Connection to existing sewer assumed | Capacity confirmation |

## 13. Fire Engineering Strategy

## 14. Sustainability, Carbon and Environmental Performance

Possible Stage 2 table:

| Theme | Stage 2 strategy | Stage 3 development required |
|---|---|---|
| Operational energy | Passive-first design with low-carbon systems | Energy model refinement |
| Embodied carbon | Compare structural frame options | Whole-life carbon assessment |
| Water | Reduce demand and manage runoff on site | Detailed calculations |

## 17. Design Options Considered

17.6 Options rejected and reasons

Example table:

| Option | Description | Advantages | Disadvantages | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Option A | Steel frame | Fast construction, long spans | Higher embodied carbon | Not preferred |
| Option B | Concrete frame | Robust, good vibration control | Slower programme | Further review |
| Option C | Hybrid timber/steel | Lower carbon potential | Fire/acoustic interfaces need study | Preferred for Stage 3 testing |

## 21. Design Risk Register

21.1 Key engineering risks
21.2 Risk owner
21.3 Likelihood and impact
21.4 Mitigation strategy
21.5 Residual risk
21.6 Action required at Stage 3

Example:

| Risk | Discipline | Impact | Mitigation | Stage 3 action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Utility capacity unknown | MEP / civil | Cost and programme risk | Early provider engagement | Confirm capacity and points of connection |
| Ground conditions not fully defined | Structural / civil | Foundation risk | Commission investigation | Update foundation strategy |
| Plant space insufficient | MEP / architecture | Spatial coordination risk | Early plant sizing | Fix plant zones and access routes |

---

## 22. Assumptions, Exclusions and Dependencies

22.1 Design assumptions
22.2 Client decisions required
22.3 Survey information still required
22.4 Specialist studies still required
22.5 Exclusions from the Stage 2 report
22.6 Dependencies on other consultants
22.7 Change-control items

This is one of the most important sections in a Stage 2 report because the design is still developing. It protects the team from presenting concept-stage thinking as technical certainty.

# Suggested Report Logic

A strong Stage 2 basis of design report should answer five questions:

1. **What are we designing?**
2. **What criteria, assumptions and constraints are shaping the design?**
3. **What engineering options were considered?**
4. **What concept has been selected and why?**
5. **What must be resolved before Stage 3 can be completed?**

The key is to avoid making the report feel like a Stage 4 technical specification. At Stage 2, the report should capture the **conceptual engineering intent**, the **basis for decision-making**, and the **risks and information needed to progress into coordinated design**.
